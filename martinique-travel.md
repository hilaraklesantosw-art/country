# Martinique: A French Caribbean Illusion

*The train from Fort-de-France to Sainte-Marie stopped at a small station called François. A man got on with a large plastic bag full of red snapper, the fish still glistening with sea water. He sat across from me, examined my face for a moment, and asked in French if I was Canadian. When I said no—Chinese, American, just passing through—he nodded as if this explained everything and offered me a piece of fish.*

---

The plane descended over the Caribbean, and for a moment the view was almost too perfect: turquoise water surrounding a green island that looked like a gemstone set in blue velvet. I'd seen photographs like this before, in travel magazines and vacation brochures, and I thought I knew what to expect. Martinique, the poster child of French sophistication in the tropics, where rum flows like water and the beaches are groomed to within an inch of their lives.

But the airport told a different story.

The terminal at Aimé Césaire International Airport was small, inefficient, and distinctly un-French in its chaos. A customs officer with bored eyes stamped my passport without looking up. The baggage carousel malfunctioned twice. And outside, the taxi stand was a free-for-all of drivers shouting destinations in a rapid-fire Creole that took me several minutes to decipher.

This was my introduction to Martinique: not the polished Caribbean fantasy sold in travel agencies, but something more complicated, more human, and ultimately more interesting.

---

## The Rumor of France

The first thing you notice in Fort-de-France is the French flag. It flies everywhere—from government buildings to schoolhouses to the smallest rum shack tucked into a hillside. Martinique is technically a department of France, which means the euro is the currency, French is the official language, and locals have the same rights and passports as someone living in Lyon or Paris. You can buy a baguette here for the same price you'd pay in a Parisian bakery, and the wine selection at the supermarket rivals anything in the 15th arrondissement.

But step away from the tourist zones, and the illusion fractures.

I took a bus from Fort-de-France to the northern town of Saint-Pierre, a journey of about an hour along a winding coastal road. The bus was aattered Renault that had clearly seen better decades, and the driver played zouk music at a volume that made conversation impossible. The passengers—a mix of Martinique Creoles, some with skin so dark they seemed to absorb the sunlight, others with features that spoke of centuries of intermixing—sang along to the songs or stared out the window with an expression I couldn't read.

In Saint-Pierre, I walked the streets looking for the famous rum distillery that had once made this town famous. The old ruins from the 1902 volcanic eruption still stood on the hillside, a skeleton of stone walls and twisted metal reclaimed by tropical vines. A local woman, perhaps seventy years old, sat on a plastic chair in front of her house, watching me photograph the ruins.

"You want to see how we really live?" she asked in French, her accent soft and melodic. "Not the tourist places. The real island."

I said yes, and she laughed.

"Everybody says that. Nobody means it."

But she pointed me toward a small restaurant near the harbor, where the fish was fresh and the rum was cheap, and where the locals actually ate.

---

## The Meal That Changed Everything

The restaurant had no sign, no menu, and no prices. Plastic tables were arranged on a cracked concrete floor, and a woman whose age I couldn't determine—from distance, she could have been fifty or seventy—stood over a large pot stirring something that smelled of coconut, thyme, and Scotch bonnet peppers.

"Accras," she said, pointing to a basket of fried cod fritters. "And the colombo—that's the goat curry. You want?"

I wanted.

The accras were crispy on the outside, soft and savory within, the cod flaking apart at the touch of a fork. The colombo was a revelation: rich, complex, with layers of spice that built slowly on the tongue. It wasn't French. It wasn't Caribbean. It was something else entirely—a cuisine that had evolved in isolation, drawing from West African traditions, Indian influences, and whatever the island's lush soil could produce.

I ate alone at my plastic table, watching the harbor. Fishing boats bobbed in the water. A group of young men played dominoes on a crate, their hands slapping the pieces down with theatrical force. An old man walked past leading a donkey laden with coconuts.

This was Martinique beyond the resort pools and the organized beach excursions. This was the island that existed when no one was watching.

---

## The Train to Nowhere

The next day, I took the little tourist train that runs along the coast between Fort-de-France and Saint-Joseph. It was a narrow-gauge railway that had once carried sugar cane to the ports; now it carried tourists like me on a ninety-minute journey through the island's interior.

My cabin-mate was a Martinique man named Jean-Philippe, perhaps sixty, with silver hair and a gold ring on his pinky finger that he kept twisting throughout the journey. He was returning from a hospital in Fort-de-France where he'd been receiving treatment for diabetes—a fact he shared with me almost immediately, along with his theory that the disease was caused by the chemical runoff from banana plantations.

"We used to be free," he said, staring out the window at the passing banana groves. "Before the French came. Then we were slaves. Then we were subjects. Now we're department. You know what that means? It means we get French social security and French passports and the right to complain in French. But the land still isn't ours. The money still goes to Paris."

The train stopped at a small station, and Jean-Philippe pointed out the window to a cluster of small houses climbing up a hillside.

"That's where I was born. My grandmother was a slave. Her mother was a slave too. Three generations, and I'm supposed to be grateful for the euro."

The landscape rolled past—green hills, banana plantations, occasional glimpses of the sea through gaps in the vegetation. It was beautiful, in a curated way, like a garden designed to be photographed. But Jean-Philippe's words had planted something in my mind, a question that wouldn't leave: What does it mean to be both French and Caribbean? To carry the passport of one nation while your roots belong to another?

---

## The Beach at Sainte-Anne

I spent my last day at the beach in Sainte-Anne, the southern tip of the island, where the water was so clear I could see fish swimming beneath the surface. It was a Sunday, and the beach was crowded with Martinique families—the children shrieking with joy, the adults lying on towels or playing beach volleyball, everyone speaking in that melodic Creole that sounds like French sung at a slightly different tempo.

I found a spot under a palm tree and watched.

A young mother chased her toddler into the surf, the child screaming with delight at the cold water. A group of teenagers played music from a portable speaker, their bodies moving with the easy rhythm that seems native to this part of the world. An elderly couple sat in folding chairs, speaking quietly to each other, their hands occasionally finding each other in the sand.

This was the Martinique I'd seen in the photographs. The turquoise water, the white sand, the palm trees swaying in the trade winds. But now I saw it differently. Now I saw it through the eyes of Jean-Philippe and the woman with the accras and the old man with the donkey. I saw a place that was both paradise and problem, both French and Creole, both a vacation destination and a home to nearly 400,000 people.

A vendor walked past selling "glaces"—ice creams in flavors like passion fruit, coconut, and rum raisin. I bought one, the sweetness cutting through the salt air, and let it melt slowly on my tongue.

---

## The Departure

My plane left in the late afternoon, and as we ascended, I looked down at the island one last time. The northern mountains were shrouded in clouds, the southern beaches sparkled in the sun, and the capital spread out along the coast like any other French city, complete with roundabouts and traffic jams.

I thought about what the woman in Saint-Pierre had said: "You want to see how we really live?" At the time, I thought she was testing me. Now I realized she was offering something—a glimpse behind the curtain, a peek at the island that exists beneath the tourism brochure.

Martinique is many things: a French department, a Caribbean island, a rum producer, a vacation destination, a complicated historical site where the legacy of slavery and colonialism remain visible in the faces of its people. It's a place where you can drink a café au lait in the morning and a ti-punch in the evening, where the language shifts between French and Creole depending on who's speaking, where the food blends African, Indian, and European influences into something uniquely its own.

It's not perfect. The economic dependency on France is real. The racial dynamics are complicated. The tourist infrastructure sometimes feels like a thin veneer over deeper issues.

But neither is it the artificial paradise depicted in the brochures. It's a real place with real people living real lives—and that's what makes it interesting.

As the plane banked north, I saw a last glimpse of the island: the green hills, the blue water, the white beaches. Then the clouds took over, and we were flying over the open sea toward Miami, carrying with us the taste of rum and colombo, the sound of zouk, and the persistent question of what it means to call a place home when that place belongs, in some ways, to another country entirely.

---

*The man with the red snapper had told me, in his broken French, that the best rum on the island wasn't the famous brands they export to France. It was the white rum you buy in plastic bottles from a man named René who lives in the hills above Sainte-Marie. I never found René's place. But I think about him sometimes, distilling rum in the Caribbean night, part of an island that refuses to be only one thing.*
