# Oman: Echoes of Desert and Sea

**Notes from a Traveler Through the Sultanate**

---

## I. Arrival in Muscat

As the plane began its descent, I looked out the window at the eastern coast of the Arabian Peninsula—a landscape of brownish-yellow land dotted with pale cities, Muscat spread out like a scattering of pearls along the coastline. This country, I told myself, would be completely different from anywhere I had been before.

The Oman Air plane slowly descended into Muscat International Airport. The moment I stepped off the escalator, a wave of heat hit me, carrying the scent of sea salt and some kind of spice I couldn't name. The airport was newer than I expected, and much quieter—no crowds, no hawkers shouting, just cool air conditioning and footsteps echoing through the vast terminal.

At immigration, the officer was a tall, thin Omani man in a white dishdasha and matching headscarf. He examined my passport carefully, looked up, and asked in English: "Alone?"

"Yes."

He nodded, stamped the page without any small talk, and handed the passport back. That restrained and polite distance was something I would encounter over and over again in the days to come.

At the currency exchange counter, I exchanged dollars for Omani rials. The rate was fixed—1 USD = 0.385 OMR—everything in this country seemed so orderly, no bargaining, no exchange rate fluctuations. An elderly exchanger counted the bills slowly and precisely, then handed me a thick stack of rials. I noticed a large silver ring on his finger, inscribed with intricate Arabic script.

I walked out of the arrivals hall and caught a yellow taxi. The driver was a Bangladeshi man from Dhaka, who had been driving in Oman for fifteen years. "Fifteen years," he said, "but I've never been to the desert." His English carried a heavy South Asian accent but was surprisingly fluent. In Oman, foreign workers form the lifeblood of the country—construction workers, hotel servers, drivers, shop assistants—it's rare to encounter an actual "Omani" serving you. This was the first lesson I learned upon arrival.

## II. The Breath of the City

Muscat is a strange city. Its old town—Muscat Old Town—still retains its 18th-century character: narrow alleys, yellow-white stone buildings, carved wooden doors and windows. But just steps away, modern high-rises rise up, shopping malls and five-star hotels line the coastal road. These two time periods are brutally spliced together, yet somehow they don't feel incongruous.

I stayed at a small hotel on the edge of the old town. The room was tiny—just a bed, a humming air conditioner, and a narrow bathroom. But it had one advantage: from the window, I could see the distant harbor and the traditional wooden sailing boats—dhows—docked there.

In the evening, I walked out of the hotel and wandered through the old town's alleys. The alleys were narrow, the narrowest just wide enough for one person, the walls golden in the sunset. The air was filled with the scent of frankincense—Oman's signature scent, an ancient trade commodity that still permeates every corner of this country. I walked into a shop selling frankincense. The shopkeeper was an elderly Omani man with a long beard. He showed me various grades of frankincense, from dark brown granules to nearly transparent yellow crystals.

"The best comes from Dhofar," he said, "in the south. You have to fly there."

I bought a small box of frankincense resin and placed it beside my bed. Lying in bed at night, the fragrance slowly drifted into my nose, and I suddenly realized—this might be my first memory of Oman: not the scenery, not the architecture, but a smell.

## III. Moving Between Cities

The next day, I decided to visit one of Oman's most famous attractions—Nizwa Fort. This massive mud-brick fortress is located in Oman's interior, built in the 13th century, and is the largest remaining fort in Oman.

From Muscat to Nizwa, there are no trains and no regular passenger buses. I had to rely on shared taxis—a very common means of transportation in Oman. The driver would wait until the car was full—four or five passengers—before starting. This method was not fast, but it was the best window into Omani society.

My fellow passengers included a young Omani engineer in a tailored suit, carrying a briefcase; an Indian construction worker, skin tanned dark, his work clothes stained with white cement; and an elderly couple, dressed simply, judging by their clothes, from a rural area. No one spoke. The driver played Arabic-language radio, the volume loud, but no one seemed to care.

The drive took about two hours. Outside the window was the same desert landscape—occasional lone acacia trees, occasionally a flock of goats grazing on a distant hillside. Suddenly, the Indian worker spoke up, asking in English where I was from. When I said China, he smiled: "My brother is also in China, in Guangzhou, doing textile business."

This was a pattern I would encounter repeatedly—Oman is a country of international workers, but their stories are rarely written about. They come to this wealthy oil country, build houses, pave roads, serve tourists, then return home with their savings. They are Oman's invisible presence.

## IV. The Fort and Souq of Nizwa

Nizwa Fort was more impressive than I expected. This massive mud-brick structure rises about thirty meters high, surrounded by high walls with numerous gun holes and defensive works. Walking inside the fort, climbing the narrow stairs to the top floor, you can see the entire city—Nizwa spread out before you, distant mountains appearing as fuzzy outlines in the heat waves.

Inside the fort are displayed ancient weapons, armor, and daily objects. Most striking was a complete set of torture devices—various tools used to punish crimes. The guide was a young Omani, explaining the uses of these tools in a monotone voice: this is for cutting off ears, this is for cutting off noses... I didn't really want to hear more.

Exiting the fort, I walked to the old city's market. This is called "Nizwa Souq"—a traditional Arabic bazaar. The entrance is a narrow arched passage, but walking inside opens up into a circular hall with two-story shop galleries surrounding it.

The souq sold all kinds of goods: copper ware, pottery, spices, carpets, traditional clothing. I stopped at a copperware stall. The stall owner was a middle-aged Omani, hammering a copper plate. When he heard I was interested in copperware, he put down his work and started promoting.

"This is hand-made," he said, "not those cheap manufactured items from China."

I noticed his English carried a distinct tribal accent. Later I learned that the area around Nizwa belongs to one of Oman's oldest tribes—the Afriqid tribe. This tribe's people are known for their bravery and have played important roles in Oman's history multiple times.

I bought a small copper plate as a gift for a friend.

## V. The Wahiba Sands

From Nizwa, heading further east, I took another shared taxi to the Wahiba Sands. This desert, located in eastern Oman, is famous for its unique dunes and Bedouin tribes.

At the village of Al Huzayyin, we switched to four-wheel-drive vehicles—essential for entering the deep desert. The driver was a Bedouin named Salem, skin dark as dark leather, but eyes exceptionally bright.

"Follow me," he said, "I'll take you to a place no one knows."

We followed his Toyota Land Cruiser over dunes, then shuttled between the dunes. The colors of the dunes surprised me—not a single golden yellow, but gradients from white to orange to deep red. Salem said it was because the desert contains different minerals.

In a secluded valley, Salem spread out a carpet and served us tea and dates. The tea was hot, sweet, with a refreshing mint flavor. The dates were black, sticky, and sweet.

"Here," Salem said, "time is different. You are always in a hurry in the city, but in the desert, you must learn to wait."

When night fell, Salem lit a fire on the dunes. The flames danced against the black sky, the surrounding dunes appearing in strange shapes in the firelight. I lay on the sand, looking up at the Milky Way—having lived in the city too long, I had forgotten what starry skies looked like.

Anna said she wanted to stay here for a few more days.

"No electricity, no signal," she said, "maybe this is what life is really like."

## VI. Searching for the Blue Eye

The next morning, I left the camp alone and walked through the desert. The sun had just risen, the dunes colored pink and gold. In the distance, I saw a figure—an elderly man in traditional white dishdasha, leading a camel toward me.

He walked up to me and stopped, examining me with curious eyes.

"Where are you from?" he asked.

"China."

He nodded, seemingly not particularly interested in the answer. Then he did something I didn't expect—he bent down, picked up something from the sand, and handed it to me.

It was a small blue glass eye—about the size of a marble we played with as children, but with concentric circles in the middle, looking like an eye.

"This is an amulet," he said, "a blue eye. In Oman, people believe it can ward off evil."

I took the amulet and examined it closely. In the sunlight, the glass eye glowed an eerie blue. The old man didn't ask for money, just smiled, then led his camel away, disappearing over the dune.

This small blue eye became the most precious souvenir of my journey. It was placed inside my backpack, accompanying me through every corner of Oman. I don't know who that old man was, where he came from, why he was in the desert. But perhaps this is the meaning of travel—you never know what's around the next corner.

## VII. Mountains and Canyons

After leaving the Wahiba Sands, I took a bus to the most famous attraction in northern Oman—Jabal Shams. This mountain is over 3,000 meters high, the highest peak on the Arabian Peninsula.

From the mountain village to the summit, there's a winding road, but buses can only reach a village called "Tiwi". From there, tourists can choose to hike or hire a four-wheel-drive vehicle to the top.

I chose to hike.

The climb was harder than I expected. The altitude rose quickly, the air became thinner, but the temperature dropped. The sun blazed on my back, but the breath I exhaled was cold.

I saw almost no other hikers along the way. Only rocks, mountain winds, and occasional lizards scrambling past. After about three hours of walking, I finally reached the summit.

Standing at the top of Jabal Shams, I felt Oman's mountains for the first time. Below me were continuous canyons—canyons 1,500 meters deep, like fissures in the earth. At the canyon bottom was green vegetation, forming a sharp contrast with the surrounding yellow rocks.

"This is called the 'Grand Canyon'," a voice came from behind me.

I turned and saw an old man sitting on a rock, with a cane beside him. He was wearing traditional Omani clothing, but had removed his headscarf, revealing gray hair.

"Are you hiking?" he asked.

"Yes."

"Good. Young people today are too lazy. They all want to take cars." He smiled, showing yellow teeth. "When I was young, I walked the mountain paths every day. From this village to that village, it took a whole day."

He told me he was seventy-eight years old, living in a village at the foot of the mountain. His family had lived here for generations.

"Has Oman changed?" I asked.

"Changed," he said, "changed too fast. Young people don't want to live in the mountains anymore. They want to go to the city, to make money. But they forgot, this mountain is our roots."

As I descended, the sun was already setting. Looking back at Jabal Shams, the summit was still lit by sunlight, glowing gold. I suddenly understood Oman's contradiction—this country tries to find balance between tradition and modernization, but inevitably loses some things.

## VIII. Conversation at the Restaurant

Returning to Muscat, I decided to visit the city's famous "Mariam Restaurant." This restaurant is located in a narrow alley of the old town, famous for its traditional Omani cuisine.

The restaurant was very small, only six or seven tables, with old photos and traditional Arabic tapestries on the walls. My host was a young Omani woman named Fatima.

"Is this your first time in Oman?" she asked, guiding me to a table.

"Yes."

"Do you like it?"

I thought about it and said: "I don't know. That's a hard question to answer."

She laughed: "A lot of people say that. They come to Oman, think everything is good, but can't say why it's good."

She recommended I try the local specialty—shuwa. This is lamb marinated with special spices and slow-roasted for an entire day, served with rice and nuts. When the dish arrived, the lamb was completely deboned, the meat so tender it melted in your mouth, carrying a complex spice flavor.

Fatima sat at the table next to me, and we started chatting. She told me she was twenty-eight, studying business management at Sultan Qaboos University, and helped at the family restaurant in her spare time.

"Omani women," she said, "are changing. We can work, we can drive, we can wear what we want. But some things are still hard."

"Like what?"

She hesitated: "Like, you can't really become a leader. In Oman, in the end, everything is decided by men."

I noticed her wording—"in Oman." She didn't say "we Omanis," though she was clearly Omani. This subtle sense of distance made me realize that perhaps Oman's identity is more complex than I thought.

## IX. The Other End of the Coastline

In the last few days in Oman, I decided to explore the eastern coast. The coastal waters there have Oman's most famous diving resources—Musandam Peninsula.

From Muscat to Musandam, you first need to take a ferry across the narrow strait, then a bus along the coastline. This route was longer than I expected—the bus moved slowly along winding mountain roads, passing through poor fishing villages and luxurious resorts at times.

My fellow passenger was an Omani government official—you could tell from his dress and bearing. He wore a well-tailored suit, a gold watch on his wrist, the latest iPhone in his hand.

"Are you here for work?" he asked.

"No, tourism."

He smiled: "Oman isn't a typical tourist country. Many people don't know what's here."

"What do you think is here?"

"History," he said, "longer history than your country. We have 5,000 years of civilization. But you only see oil."

There was a hint of discontent in his tone. I tried to explain that my understanding of Oman wasn't just about oil. But the words caught in my throat. Maybe he was right—作为游客 I found it difficult to truly understand this country's complexity.

The beaches of Musandam Peninsula were unexpectedly beautiful. Crystal clear waters, white sand, lonely palm trees. But what impressed me most were the villagers of Musandam—they looked completely different from the Omanis I had met before. Their skin was darker, their language carried a different accent—this was an independent community with its own traditions.

I stayed at a small inn in the village, run by a quiet elderly man. He barely spoke English, most of our communication was through gestures and smiles. But one night, he suddenly spoke, telling me his family story in Arabic. Although I couldn't understand, I could feel the depth of emotion in his words.

## X. Notes of Departure

On the morning I left Oman, I woke up early and wandered through Muscat's old town. The early morning old town was almost empty, only a few cats shuttling through the alleys. In the harbor, dhow sailing boats sat quietly, waiting for the moment to set sail.

I stood by the sea, watching the sun rise from the horizon, turning the entire city gold. Suddenly, I heard the call to prayer echoing in the distance—from a mosque's loudspeaker, reverberating through the entire city.

At that moment, I felt a strange peace. This country, this one I never thought I would come to, accepted me in its own way—although I could never fully understand it.

At the airport, I met the German girl Anna I had encountered in the desert. We exchanged contact information, then walked toward different boarding gates.

"How do you feel about Oman?" she asked—the same question as before.

"I don't know," I said, "maybe I need a long time to answer that question."

She smiled: "Perhaps that's Oman's magic. It makes you question yourself, rather than giving you answers."

## Epilogue: The Amulet

Now, that small blue glass eye sits on my desk. It's only thumb-sized, glowing an eerie blue in the sunlight. Every time I see it, I think of that old man in the desert, the camel, those dunes, and that country I can never fully understand.

Oman is not a place that will make you fall in love with it. It's too quiet, too restrained, too distant. But it will make you think—about time, about tradition, about change, about our place in this world.

Perhaps this is what Paul Theroux meant by the true meaning of travel: not to find answers, but to ask questions. Not to praise the scenery, but to understand people. Not to prove anything, but to discover your own ignorance.

The last thing Oman taught me is: there are some places you leave, but can never truly leave.
